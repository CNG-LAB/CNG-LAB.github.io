Shaping Brain Structure

Welcome to the website of the Otto Hahn Group: Cognitive Neurogenetics.
We investigate how genes and environment shape the function and structure of the cortex. We are situated at the Max Planck Institute for Human Cognitive and Brain Sciences and have strong ties with FZ Jülich, INM-7.

We want to understand how the individual mind arises, and do so by studying the interplay of genetic and environmental factors on organization of brain structure and function. We develop and apply a mixture of interdisciplinary approaches that integrate neuroimaging, genetics, and cognitive science with various behavioural and health markers. We approach our research question from three complementary angles, that enhance eachother.




1. Multi-modal (brain) Maps

Structural and functional characteristics of the cortex systematically vary along global axes as a function of cytoarchitecture, gene expression, and connectivity. The organized topology of the cerebral cortex has a strong relationship with human cognition and is associated with the impact and progression of disorder. We study such axes, and investigate the interplay between gradual transitions across the cortex on the one hand, and brain regions with clear borders on the other.

2. Brain and Behavior

Evaluating the systematic relationship between the brain organization and behavior. Over the past decades, various studies have shown relationships between variation in brain structure and function and behaviour. Moreover, macro-anatomical features and behaviour have been observed to show domain-specific change as a consequence of training. We study the genetic and environmental basis of brain-behaviour relationships to further understand the systematic relationship between brain organization and cognition, and the role of health factors such as sleep and bloodpressure.

3. Phylo- and ontogeny

Not only do genes and environment shape brain structure and function, time is also an essitial factor. Therefor, we study change over time, as well as development/ageing, and evolutionary development, to further understand the balance between brain and time.
