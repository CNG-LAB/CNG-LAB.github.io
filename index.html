UNDER CONSTRUCTION! The nature and nurture of brain and behavior

Welcome to the website of the Otto Hahn Group: Cognitive Neurogenetics.
We are situated at the Max Planck Institute for Human Cognitive and Brain Sciences and have strong ties with FZ Jülich, INM-7.

Our research seeks to understand how heritable and environmental factors shape brain structure and function, where we are especially curious about regions supporting integrative thought, such as social cognition and emotion. We develop and apply various interdisciplinary approaches that integrate neuroimaging, genetics, and non-brain phenotypes combining theory- and data-driven approaches. Our multi-level research rests on three complementary themes that address the interplay of nature and nuture on brain and behavior in healthy and diseased brains.




1. Mapping

The nature and nurture of brain organization: Structural and functional characteristics of the cortex systematically vary along global axes as a function of cytoarchitecture, gene expression, and connectivity. The organized topology of the cerebral cortex has a strong relationship with human cognition and is associated with the impact and progression of disorder. We study such axes, and investigate the interplay between gradual transitions across the cortex on the one hand, and brain regions with clear borders on the other.

2. Individuals

Evaluating the systematic relationship between the brain organization and behavior. Over the past decades, various studies have shown relationships between variation in brain structure and function and behaviour. Moreover, macro-anatomical features and behaviour have been observed to show domain-specific change as a consequence of training. We study the genetic and environmental basis of brain-behaviour relationships to further understand the systematic relationship between brain organization and cognition, and the role of health factors such as sleep and bloodpressure.

3. Time

Studying the relationship between the organization of the brain in ontogeny and phylogeny. Reproducibility and reliability are important features in science. Therefor we continuously validate and evaluate our brain markers and observations. For example, we test the reliability of cortical thickness measurements across different pipelines. Moreover, we try to develop framework and methods to understand causes between-sample variations.
