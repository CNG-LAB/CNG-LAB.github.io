The nature and nurture of brain and behavior

Welcome to the website of the Otto Hahn Group: Cognitive Neurogenetics.
We are situated at the Max Planck Institute for Human Cognitive and Brain Sciences and have strong ties with FZ Jülich, INM-7.

Our research seeks to understand how heritable and environmental factors shape brain structure and function. We develop and apply various interdisciplinary approaches that integrate neuroimaging, genetics, and non-brain phenotypes using various machine learning approaches. Our multi-level research rests on three complementary themes that address the interplay of nature and nuture on brain and behavior in healthy and diseased brains.




THEME 1: Uncovering the interrelationship between natural brain axes and cognition in health and disorder

Structural and functional characteristics of the cortex systematically vary along global axes as a function of cytoarchitecture, gene expression, and connectivity. The organized topology of the cerebral cortex has a strong relationship with human cognition and is associated with the impact and progression of disorder. We study such axis, and investigate how these natural axes relate to behaviour and disorder.

THEME 2. Dissecting individual differences

Over the past decades, various studies have shown relationships between variation in brain structure and function and behavioural skills. Moreover, macro-anatomical features and behaviour have been observed to change as a consequence of training. We study the genetic and environmental basis of brain-behaviour relationships to further understand the relationship between brain and non-brain phenotypes such as sleep, blood pressure, and personality.

THEME 3. Biomarker validation

Reproducibility and reliability are important features in science. Therefor we continuously validate and evaluate our brain markers and observations. For example, we test the reliability of cortical thickness measurements across different pipelines. Moreover, we try to develop framework and methods to understand causes between-sample variations.
